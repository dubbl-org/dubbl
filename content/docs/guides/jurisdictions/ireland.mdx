## Overview

Ireland is one of Europe's most attractive jurisdictions for business incorporation, particularly for technology, pharmaceutical, and IP-heavy companies. Its 12.5% corporate tax rate on trading income has been a cornerstone of its appeal for decades, and the country hosts European headquarters for many of the world's largest companies including Apple, Google, and Meta. As an English-speaking EU member with a common law legal system, Ireland offers a familiar and transparent business environment with full access to the EU single market.

## Entity Types Available

- **Private Company Limited by Shares (Ltd)** - The most common structure, suitable for most businesses. Requires a minimum of 1 director (but see residency requirements below) and 1 shareholder
- **Designated Activity Company (DAC)** - Similar to Ltd but with a defined objects clause limiting activities. Requires minimum 2 directors
- **Public Limited Company (PLC)** - For larger companies intending to offer shares to the public. Minimum share capital of EUR 25,000
- **Unlimited Company (ULC)** - No limit on members' liability but offers privacy advantages as financial statements may not need to be filed publicly

## Tax Structure

| Item | Rate |
|------|------|
| Corporate tax (trading income) | 12.5% |
| Corporate tax (Pillar Two in-scope) | 15% for groups with global revenue above EUR 750m |
| Corporate tax (non-trading/passive income) | 25% |
| Capital gains tax | 33% |
| R&D tax credit | 30% of qualifying expenditure |
| Knowledge Development Box (KDB) | Effective 6.25% on qualifying IP income |
| VAT (standard rate) | 23% |
| Dividend withholding tax | 25% (reduced under treaties) |

**Key points:**

- The 12.5% trading rate applies to active business income - one of the lowest in the EU
- Pillar Two (global minimum tax) applies only to large multinational groups with consolidated revenue above EUR 750 million
- The R&D tax credit of 30% can be used to offset corporation tax or claimed as a cash refund over three years
- The Knowledge Development Box provides an effective 6.25% rate on profits from qualifying patents and copyrighted software
- Ireland has an extensive double tax treaty network with 70+ countries
- No thin capitalisation rules, though transfer pricing rules apply to related party transactions
- Participation exemption available for gains on disposal of qualifying shareholdings

## Setup Process and Costs

1. **Choose a company name** and check availability with the Companies Registration Office (CRO)
2. **Appoint directors** - minimum 1 director for an Ltd. At least 1 director must be resident in an EEA member state, or the company must hold a Section 137 bond (approximately EUR 2,000)
3. **Appoint a company secretary** (can be a director but not the sole director if only 1 director)
4. **Prepare the Constitution** (replaces the old Memorandum and Articles of Association)
5. **Register with the CRO** using Form A1
6. **Register for tax** with Revenue (corporation tax, VAT, employer taxes as needed)
7. **Open a bank account**

**Minimum share capital:** EUR 1 (no minimum capital requirement in practice for Ltd companies)

**Total setup cost: $500-1,000** for standard incorporation. Add approximately EUR 2,000 if a Section 137 bond is needed for non-EEA resident directors.

**Timeline:** 5-10 business days for standard CRO processing. Fast-track options available.

| Permanent TSB | Traditional | Smaller but competitive for business accounts |
| Stripe Treasury | Embedded finance | Available for platform businesses using Stripe |

**Banking tips:** Irish banks have straightforward onboarding for locally incorporated companies. Remote account opening is increasingly available through digital banks. Traditional banks may require an in-person visit or Irish correspondence address. Expect standard KYC documentation requirements.

## Annual Compliance Requirements

- **CRO Annual Return** - Must be filed within 56 days of the annual return date (initially 6 months after incorporation, then annually). Late filing results in loss of audit exemption for 2 years
- **Corporation Tax Return (CT1)** - Due within 9 months of the accounting period end
- **Preliminary tax payments** - Small companies pay in one installment; larger companies pay in two
- **Financial statements** - Must be annexed to the annual return. Audit exemption available for small companies (turnover under EUR 12 million, balance sheet under EUR 6 million, fewer than 50 employees)
- **VAT returns** - Bi-monthly for most businesses
- **Employer returns** - Monthly payroll reporting under PAYE Modernisation if you have Irish employees
- **Beneficial ownership register** - Must be filed with the Register of Beneficial Ownership (RBO)

## Pros and Cons

| Pro/Con | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Pro | 12.5% corporate tax rate on trading income - among the lowest in the EU |
| Pro | R&D tax credit (30%) and Knowledge Development Box (6.25%) for IP-heavy businesses |
| Pro | English-speaking EU member with common law legal system |
| Pro | Access to the EU single market and passporting for financial services |
| Pro | Highly educated, English-speaking workforce with strong tech talent pool |
| Pro | Low setup cost ($500-1,000) and minimal capital requirements |
| Con | 25% tax on passive/non-trading income is significantly higher than the trading rate |
| Con | At least 1 EEA-resident director required, or a Section 137 bond must be obtained |
| Con | 33% capital gains tax is relatively high |
| Con | Cost of living and office space in Dublin is expensive |
| Con | Dividend withholding tax of 25% applies unless reduced by treaty |
| Con | Pillar Two may reduce the tax advantage for very large multinationals |

## Best For

Ireland is ideal for **technology companies, pharmaceutical firms, and IP-heavy businesses** looking to establish a credible EU headquarters with a low effective tax rate. The combination of 12.5% trading rate, 30% R&D credit, and 6.25% effective KDB rate makes it particularly compelling for companies that develop software or hold patents. It is also well-suited for US companies seeking an EU base, financial services firms needing EU passporting, and any business that values an English-speaking, common law jurisdiction within the European Union.
